## Voiceover script

What's up, pirates! Today we're going past the beginner stuff. You already know what holds. Luffy, Boa, anniversary sets, extra boosters and PRB set.

**But That doesn't mean you should buy anything right now.**

I'm giving you **six advanced investing tips** that separate people who actually keep money from people who buy anything that merely meets the beginner rules.

Stick around — because **tip number one** is the one almost everyone skips, and it's the one that cost me the most when I ignored it.

Let's get into it.

**Point 1 — Ask about liquidity before you ask if the set is good.**

Everyone starts with: *Is this Luffy? Is this a winner set? Will OP-17 profit?*

I start with: **If I buy this today, can I sell it before the next thing I actually need money for?**

That's liquidity. Not "will it go up someday?" It's whether you can get out without having to hard-sell the product or take a massive discount off market price.

OP-17 drops **August 28, 2026**. Fourth anniversary. Global simultaneous release. First time JP and EN launch together. You know what happens when the next big set shows up — attention moves, preorders start, and people who went all-in on the last set suddenly need cash they don't have.

So before you buy OP-16 — or any box — ask yourself this out loud:

> *"If I buy this set and OP-17 is next, will I be able to sell OP-16 **before** attention and cash rotate — or am I locking money I might need?"*

If you're **just starting out** and you don't have spare liquid cash sitting aside, even a "sure" OP-16 play becomes an **opportunity cost** problem. You're not wrong about the set. You're wrong about **timing your balance sheet**.

I'll be upfront — I've bought strong sets while broke on cash, and when OP-17 preorder season hit I had two bad choices: sell OP-16 at a discount because I was rushed, or miss OP-17 because my money was trapped in cardboard. Neither feels good.

**Liquidity rule in one line:** Good thesis + bad timing + no exit = you're not investing, you're parking money you can't use.

Luffy on the card doesn't change that math.

## Point 2 — You don't know what phase you're in

**Point 2 — Most people lose money because they don't know what phase they're in.**

And I'm not talking about OP-16 meta phase. I mean **your** phase as an investor.

Are you in **BUILD** mode — still trying to grow the pile, flip modern, learn the game, stack wins?

Or **PROTECT** mode — you already have wins on the shelf and your job is to not give them back on every preorder?

The same move can be genius in one phase and stupid in the other.

Selling a vintage box to fund OP-17 when you're up big? In BUILD mode, maybe that's offense. In PROTECT mode, that's trading down.

Buying three OP-16 cases when you only own one EB product? BUILD might justify it. PROTECT probably doesn't.

I'm breaking this down properly in **another video** — phases, when to switch, and the mistakes I see at each stage. If you haven't watched my **Strategic Rebalancing** or **Ten Year Plan** videos, watch those after this so the phase idea clicks.

For today, just remember: **if you don't know your phase, every tip in this video will feel contradictory.** That's not the tips failing. That's you skipping self-awareness.

## Point 3 — Befriend your local shop (they're your market)

**Point 3 — Your local game store owner is not optional — they're part of your investing infrastructure.**

I know online is easy. TCGPlayer, eBay, Discord. But when you need to **move** product fast — especially sealed, promos, or stuff that doesn't list well online — your network is your liquidity.

Shop owners buy. Shop owners sell to you at fair prices. Shop owners hear things before Twitter does — restock waves, allocation cuts, which promos locals are actually chasing.

When I started, I treated LGS like a retail counter. That was dumb. The guys who do this for years treat the shop like **community + pipeline**:

- **Buyers** when you need cash without shipping headaches
- **Suppliers** when allocation hits and online is dry
- **Signal** when a set is moving locally even if TCGPlayer looks flat

It's hard to sell at a fair price when nobody knows your name. Walk in cold with five OP-16 boxes during OP-17 hype week and you're begging. Walk in as someone who supports locals year-round and you'll get a conversation, not a lowball.

I'm not saying get taken advantage of. I'm saying **relationships are liquidity** in a hobby market. Alpha isn't always a Discord leak. Sometimes it's the owner telling you, *"We're not moving normal OP-15 boxes but anniversary promos are gone in a day."*

That's worth more than another YouTube top-ten list.

## Point 4 — Focus beats shiny objects

**Point 4 — Shiny object syndrome will drain your cash and your gains.**

One Piece runs hot. Then Dragon Ball pops. Naruto launches. Pokémon cools off and money rotates. Suddenly everyone's a multi-TCG fund manager with $400 a month and four hobbies.

Diversification is real. I'm not saying never touch another game.

I'm saying: **when you're starting out, focus on one lane.**

One game. One plan. One skill stack — learning English sealed behavior, reprint patterns, which characters actually hold, how your local market moves.

Jumping ship the week another TCG trends on YouTube is how you sell One Piece at a local low to buy Dragon Ball at a hype high. You feel productive. You're actually just **paying tuition twice**.

I genuinely think One Piece still has runway for western collectors who understand the IP and the reprint rules. But you don't get that edge if you're restarting from zero every time a new game trends.

**Focus rule:** Master exit timing and liquidity in **one** TCG before you brag about being "diversified." Diversification without skill is just expensive distraction.

## Point 5 — Cash isn't trash until you need it

**Point 5 — "Cash is trash" until bear market hits and you're the one forced to sell.**

A lot of us — me included — have gone **all in** on the market. On paper the portfolio looks great. OP-13 up. A few SPs graded. Sealed on the shelf. Screenshots everywhere.

In reality? **Broke.**

Not bankrupt. Just **illiquid**. Every dollar is in product. Then something shifts — reprint headline, singles cool off for a few weeks, you overbought launch week — and you need money for life or for the next buy window.

That's when people sell at **50% of market** because they're rushed, not because the card died.

Emergency cash isn't just for rent and groceries — though obviously cover those first. It's also **investing ammo** when the dip you waited for actually shows up.

If all your money is in OP-16 at $200 a box and Sakazuki manga pulls back 20% on a reprint rumor, you want to be the buyer — not the guy listing on TCGPlayer at a loss because he has no cash left.

**Paper gains aren't gains.** They're a story until you sell — or until you survive a down cycle without panic.

My rule now: I keep a **cash buffer** separate from my card budget. Not sexy. Not content-friendly. Keeps me from becoming my own worst liquidity problem.

## Point 6 — Your geography decides what you should own

**Point 6 — The market you're in changes what you should buy.**

TCGPlayer is not the whole world. It's a **reference**, not your guaranteed exit price.

**Philippines example** — smaller market, fewer buyers, more price sensitivity. Normal booster boxes can be **harder to move** than anniversary product, sealed promos, or chase singles people actually recognize. You might list at "market" and wait. Realized price often lands around **70–80%** of what US sellers get if you're patient — less if you're rushed.

**US example** — way more movement on English sealed and singles. Liquidity is better. But try dumping **Japanese** product locally and you'll feel the friction fast. EN tournaments, EN buyers, EN Facebook groups — JP is a different buyer pool.

So the advanced tip isn't "buy what Twitter likes." It's:

> **Prioritize what actually moves where you live.**

If you're in PH, overweighting random OP-15 boxes because a US YouTuber said they're sleepers might trap you. Anniversary sets, promos, recognizable Straw Hat chase — stuff a local buyer **gets** without a lecture — often clears faster.

If you're in the US, English sealed and EN SP and up usually make more sense than importing JP unless you already have JP buyers lined up.

I buy for **my** exit market, not for a screenshot that looks good in someone else's country.

## Framework — One question before every buy

Before you click buy on anything — OP-16 box, OP-17 preorder, that Luffy SP you've stared at for a week — run this checklist:

1. **Liquidity** — Can I sell this before the next major set or cash need?
2. **Phase** — Am I in BUILD or PROTECT? Does this move match that?
3. **Network** — Do I have a real buyer path, or only a listing?
4. **Focus** — Am I buying this because it fits my plan, or because another game trended?
5. **Cash** — If this goes flat for 90 days, am I still comfortable?
6. **Geography** — Will this move **here**, at a price I'd accept?

Miss one? You can still win. Miss three? You're gambling with extra steps.
