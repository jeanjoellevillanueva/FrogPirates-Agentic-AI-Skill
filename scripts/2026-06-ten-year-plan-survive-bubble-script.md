## Voiceover script

What's up, pirates! Today we're going to talk about the One Piece bubble — or at least, what everyone keeps calling a bubble.

Everyone's asking if One Piece TCG is in a bubble. **Wrong question.**

The right question is: **if** it cools, which cards still make sense on a **10-year hold** — and which ones get left behind?

I'm not giving you a price list. I'm giving you a **plan** — five rules for what belongs in a 10-year stack, **when** to enter, and **how** to exit without blowing up your account.

NOTE: I'm talking **English** cards, **Straw Hat and Boa** characters, **SP and above**. That's the lane. If you're investing with a day job and a few hundred a month, this is the framework.

Stick around — because most collectors get the **exit** wrong even when they pick the right card.

Let's get into it.

---

## Part 1 — The five rules

Let me break it down.

---

### Rule 1 — Character pillar: Straw Hat + Boa only

**Rule 1 — Straw Hat and Boa. That's it.**

This is the filter I use on every long hold. **Luffy. Zoro. Nami. Sanji. Robin. Chopper. Usopp. Franky. Brook. Jinbe.** And **Boa Hancock.**

Not "this admiral might pop." Not "this side character had a good arc." Not "film-only hype."

Ten years from now, the anime is still the Straw Hat story. Collectors still want the crew — and Boa sits in that same permanent demand bucket for this game.

I know what you're thinking — *what about Ace? Blackbeard? Shanks?*

Hear me out. Those can be great **trades**. Great **2-year plays**. But my **10-year plan** is narrow on purpose. Straw Hat plus Boa. If it doesn't pass that test, it's not in the decade stack. Full stop.

---

### Rule 2 — Chase tier only: SP and above

**Rule 2 — SP and above. No exceptions for a 10-year hold.**

Manga rares. SPs. Fixed-run promos. Tournament promos with real scarcity. That's the tier.

Not bulk secret rares everyone pulls. Not "maybe this SR spikes." Not commons you want to grade because PSA got expensive.

If you're putting money away for ten years, you're buying **chase treatment** — the art people frame, the pull rate people remember, the card type that still has a story when the meta is dead.

Mid-tier slabbing is a **2026 trap**, not a decade plan.

---

### Rule 3 — Supply class: know how scarcity actually works

**Rule 3 — Same character, different supply story.**

A Straw Hat SP and a Straw Hat manga rare can both pass Rule 1 and Rule 2 — but they're **not the same bet.**

**SP** — case-level scarcity. Roughly one per case of boxes, sometimes shared slots with other chase types. Supply grows as cases get opened.

**Manga rare** — pack-level lottery. Brutal pull math. Supply grows slower, but hype at launch is violent.

**Promo** — fixed distribution. Can't pull it from a booster. Store events, gift products, participation awards. Different risk, different liquidity.

Before you buy, ask: *how does this card get into the market?* Opening volume, reprint waves, and grading pauses all hit **different** supply classes differently.

OP-16 forward, with PSA's **eighty-dollar floor** and backlog, pop counts on new slabs will look **weird** for a while. That's a supply-class problem — not proof your card is rare forever.

---

### Rule 4 — English liquidity

**Rule 4 — If you can't sell it in English, it's not a plan — it's a display piece.**

I'm an **English-first** investor. Western tournaments, Western marketplaces, Western buyers.

Before a 10-year buy, run the liquidity check:

- Does this card trade on **TCGPlayer** with real sold volume — not just one listing?
- If you grade it, is there a **PSA 10** comp that actually sells — or only asks?
- Can you exit in **one to two weeks** without fire-saleing — or are you stuck waiting for the one buyer?

A card can be beautiful and still be **illiquid**. Illiquid plus ten years equals trapped money.

English path. Real comps. Real buyers. That's Rule 4.

---

### Rule 5 — The bubble test

**Rule 5 — If it dropped forty percent, would you still want it?**

Be honest. Not "I'd hold because I hate losing." Would you **still** be happy owning this piece in a colder market?

If **yes** — Straw Hat or Boa, SP or above, supply story you understand, English liquidity — that's a **10-year candidate.**

If **no** — it's a **trade**. Buy it to sell it. Take profit on rules, not vibes. Nothing wrong with trading. Just don't call a trade a decade hold because the pull felt exciting.

That's the whole filter:

**Straw Hat or Boa. SP and above. Know supply class. English liquidity. Pass the bubble test.**

---

## Part 2 — Entry strategy (when to buy)

Alright — you passed the rules. **When** do you actually pull the trigger?

---

### Entry 1 — Never launch week

**Entry 1 — Do not build a 10-year position in launch week.**

Week one is hype tax. Preorders, box breaks, YouTube FOMO — prices are lying to you.

My window for English sets: **week three to week eight** after release. Let the opening volume hit. Let the first reprint rumors flush weak hands. Let the floor form.

OP-16 taught a lot of people this the hard way. Triple manga hype on day one is not where I want my decade entry.

---

### Entry 2 — Buy the boredom

**Entry 2 — Best entries feel boring.**

Post-reprint dip. Flat market weeks. Post-anniversary cooldown when nobody's making videos about the set.

June twenty twenty-six — OP-13 and EB-03 reprints rolling — that's **noise** for flippers and **opportunity** for planners. Reprint fear hits sealed and chase cards. If your card passes the five rules, boredom is often cheaper than hype.

I genuinely think the smart money buys when the timeline feels dead — not when everyone's screaming must-buy.

---

### Entry 3 — DCA, don't lump

**Entry 3 — Fixed monthly amount beats one emotional purchase.**

One hundred to two hundred dollars a month. Same thesis. Twelve to eighteen months.

You don't need to nail the bottom. You need to **not** nail the top. Stack cash monthly, then deploy on **buy zones** — not because a box break looked hot on YouTube.

---

### Entry 4 — Singles over boxes

**Entry 4 — Target the card, don't gamble the box.**

For a 10-year hold, you're buying **the** SP or **the** manga — not hoping a box respects you.

Pull math on manga is brutal — community estimates often land around **one per two to six cases**. SP slots compete with other chase types. Box EV for hitting **your** card is negative for most people.

One box for fun? Fine. **Decade stack?** Singles after the floor forms.

---

### Entry 5 — One sealed 10-year add per quarter

**Entry 5 — Discipline beats conviction. Sealed edition.**

Maximum **one** new **sealed** 10-year position per quarter. If the product doesn't clear the five rules, you **wait**.

That means **English** booster boxes on sets with real **Straw Hat or Boa** chase DNA in the pool — or sealed **Extra Boosters** and premium products Bandai doesn't reprint. Bought at **MSRP, allocation, or buy zone** — not launch-week scalper tax.

This stops sealed portfolio creep — twelve random boxes from every set because FOMO said must-buy. I'd rather own **four** sealed products I trust for ten years than twenty maybes gathering dust in the closet.

One box per quarter. Thesis clear. Price disciplined. That's how working investors actually build a sealed stack without turning their apartment into a warehouse.

---

## Part 3 — Exit strategy (how to get out)

Ten-year hold does **not** mean never sell. It means you exit on **rules**, not panic.

---

### Exit 1 — Predefined target before you buy

**Exit 1 — Set the number when you enter.**

Two-x after fees. Sell half at a fixed dollar amount. Whatever fits your life — but **write it down** before you own the card.

No target means you'll hold through a double, watch it round-trip, and call it "long-term investing." That's not a plan. That's cope.

---

### Exit 2 — Sell into strength

**Exit 2 — Take profit when the market gives you a gift.**

Holiday window — **November, December** — liquidity and gift buying pick up. Post-tournament spikes when a Straw Hat deck tops an event. Viral anime moment when casual money floods in.

You don't have to sell everything. But **trimming into strength** is how working investors actually realize gains without quitting their jobs to watch charts.

---

### Exit 3 — Trim, don't dump

**Exit 3 — Sell twenty-five to fifty percent, let the rest ride.**

If the five rules still hold — Straw Hat or Boa, chase tier, supply story intact, English liquidity, bubble test still yes — the remainder can stay in the decade bucket.

This is how you pay yourself **and** keep upside. All-or-nothing exits are for trades, not core holds.

---

### Exit 4 — Cut when the thesis breaks

**Exit 4 — Exit clean when the story changes.**

Wrong character — you broke Rule 1. Bought a side character SP because it spiked. **Cut it.**

Supply flood on **your** treatment — not generic reprint panic, but real volume hitting your exact card type. Re-evaluate.

Life happens — you need cash. Sell the **weakest** 10-year name, not your best one, unless everything has to go.

Don't average down on hope. The decade plan is strict so you don't bag-hold for twelve years on a mistake.

---

### Exit 5 — Use English liquidity on the way out

**Exit 5 — Know your fee math before you list.**

TCGPlayer seller fees. Shipping. Grading sunk cost. PayPal or platform cuts on other venues. That "profit" might be **thirteen percent** thinner than you think.

List with comps from **sold** data. Tracked shipping on anything over two hundred. Verified buyers when you can.

You did the work on entry with Rule 4. Don't throw it away on a sloppy exit because you got impatient.

**Don't sell on reprint rumors alone.** Check supply class. A main-set reprint doesn't always kill a promo. Panic is free. Discipline isn't.

---

## Closing stack

You don't need twenty sealed boxes and twenty singles.

You need **one sealed 10-year add per quarter** — plus singles only when you're targeting a specific chase — and everything passes the test:

**Straw Hat or Boa. SP and above. Supply class you understand. English liquidity. Bubble test.**

Buy boring — week three to eight, post-reprint dips, DCA, singles for the card / sealed for the stack.

Exit on rules — target set, trim into strength, cut when the thesis breaks.

If the market cools, you're not guessing which cards survive. You already decided — before you clicked buy.
